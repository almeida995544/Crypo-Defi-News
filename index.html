Why Crypto Hackers Always Get Caught
15-3-22
Since the early days of cryptocurrency, hackers have been one of the biggest threats to the space. As markets have grown, the security of cryptocurrencies has become one of the most significant sources of discomfort for investors. After several high-profile heists on exchanges like Bitfinex and Mt.Gox, the popular wisdom in the crypto space was that you should store funds in a non-custodial wallet. But even they are not necessarily safe. If white hat hackers can penetrate a hardware wallet to restore lost encryption, you can bet a black hat hacker would be ready to exploit the same weaknesses.
In some cases, funds are quickly restored, such as the recent Wormhole Bridge attack. Project operators were quick to discover that a generous bounty for bugs often proves more attractive than keeping illicit funds, which is the ideal outcome from a user's point of view. However, if the authorities get involved, theft victims can wait months or even years to restore their stolen cryptocurrencies. After the DoJ recently arrested two people in connection with the laundering of stolen Bitfinex funds, it appears that those affected may face a legal dispute to recoup their losses.

What is a DAO?
A DAO, or Decentralized Autonomous Organization, is an online organization that exists and operates without a single leader or governing body. DAOs are run by code written on a blockchain like Ethereum (ETH) and are owned and operated by the people who use them.
There are many different types of DAOs, but they all have one thing in common: they are decentralized, which means that decisions about the organization's future are decided by the collective group rather than a single individual.
This decentralization is what makes DAOs promising, as it theoretically eliminates the possibility of corruption or manipulation by a single entity. Smart contracts (not people) enforce the organization's terms and conditions, making them incredibly efficient and resilient to change.
